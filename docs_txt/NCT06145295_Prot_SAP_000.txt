The experiment group (the mobile online support group)


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
In the experiment group, participants studied the patient education materials that


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
provided in both word content and video format by themselves. Weekly reminder was


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
sent to remind patients to study one of the education materials. The video format


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
education materials were presented and video recorded by the healthcare professionals


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
which each last around 10 minutes. Inspired by the Group 4 Health workshop rooted in


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
offline support group, comprise four mandatory courses derived from scientific research


[Title] (page 3)
on physical, emotional, and role management knowledge relevant to the rehabilitation of


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
ovarian cancer patients (Graziani & Tsakos, 2020). These materials underwent a


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
comprehensive review process involving researchers and healthcare professionals,


[NarrativeText] (page 3)

[NarrativeText] (page 3)

[NarrativeText] (page 3)
This thorough review aimed to ensure the content's reliability and readability. The first


[NarrativeText] (page 3)

[NarrativeText] (page 3)

[NarrativeText] (page 3)
memberships. In the third and fourth classes, participants explored ways to enhance


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
existing meaningful group memberships and acquire new ones, respectively.


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
To nurture a sense of belonging and enhance peer support between patients, the


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
online support group provided online community function to enables patients to share


[NarrativeText] (page 3)

[NarrativeText] (page 3)

[NarrativeText] (page 3)

The symptom recording feature aligned with the health-related quality of life

[Title] (page 3)

Patients recorded symptoms on a three-point scale ranging from "no symptoms" to

[NarrativeText] (page 3)

[NarrativeText] (page 3)

Measure


[Title] (page 4)
Identity synthesis (primary outcome)


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
The measure of recovery identity included the assessment of patients' acceptance


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
of their illness identity and their self-esteem derived from their social identities. The


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
acceptance of illness identity was measured using the Illness Identity Questionnaire's


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
acceptance sub-scale, consisting of 6 items (Oris et al., 2016). The maintenance of former


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
social identities was measured using the Maintenance of Group Memberships sub-scale of


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
meaningful social identity was measured using the New Group Memberships sub-scale of


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
the Exeter Identity Transition Scales, which includes 4 questions. Responses were rated


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
on a scale from 1 to 5, with higher scores indicating a higher level of acceptance of the


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
identity. The recovery identity is calculated as the sum of each average score of


[Title] (page 4)
acceptance of illness identity, maintenance of former identity and new identity.


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
Health-related quality of life (secondary outcome)


[Title] (page 5)
HRQOL was measured using the Short Form Health Survey 12 (SF-12)


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
(Jakobusson, 2007), which includes twelve questions. Each item is transformed into a


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
scale ranging from 0 to 100 according to a specific scoring algorithm. These scores are


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
then aggregated to generate two summary scores: the Physical Component Summary


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
(PCS) and the Mental Component Summary (MCS). The resulting scores are norm-based,


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
comparing an individual's health status to population norms. HRQOL is calculate as the


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
summary of PCS and MCS. Higher scores indicate better HRQOL, with scores around 50


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
representing the average.


[Title] (page 5)
Participant Engagement


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
In the experimental group, the peer support was calculated based on system-


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
recorded time spent on relevant pages, while health record and patient education


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
involvement were gauged by the frequency of actions, encompassing symptom recording


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
and engagement with educational materials. Conversely, in the control group, participant


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
engagement was evaluated by calculating peer support based on session duration. Health


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
record involvement was quantified by the number of symptom and emotion record


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
entries, and patient education engagement was measured by the number of attended


[Title] (page 5)
lessons.

Data collection and analysis


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
Data collection occurred at three time points: before the intervention, immediately


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
after the intervention, and at a three-month follow-up. Questionnaires were administered


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
through a website link hosted on Sojump, and data were stored in Excel and analyzed


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
using SPSS 26.0. The determination of the sample size involved a statistical power


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
analysis, considering a two-sided test with a significance level (α = 0.05) and a desired


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
statistical power of 80% (1-β). A minimum of 34 individuals per intervention arm was


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
deemed necessary.

2.1 Background (Research Question, Available Data from the literature, Current strategy for dealing with the problem, Rationale of the research that paves the way to the aim(s) of the work). (200-250 words max.)


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
Pilonidal disease derives its name from Latin- pilus meaning “hair,” and nidus meaning “nest” (1). The source of


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
pilonidal disease is thought to be a deep intergluteal sulcus. It is widely accepted that the establishment of the pilonidal


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
sinus results from the penetration of shed hair shafts through the skin, which ultimately leads to an acute or chronic


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
infected site (2).


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
Pilonidal disease is largely considered a surgical disease, especially in acute instances with secondary infection and


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
abscess. Infection or abscess requires incision and drainage. Definitive treatment is delayed the majority of the time if


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
there is an acute infection or abscess until after the infection has been addressed. Surgical options for chronic disease are


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
numerous and can include “pit picking,” curettage, aspiration, unroofing, or surgical excision. Defects can be closed


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
primarily, with flaps or grafts, or allowed to heal by secondary intention (3-5).


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
The most serious problem of the various surgical approaches proposed is the recurrence rate, ranging from 0% to 40% (6,


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
The surgical treatment of patients with recurrent disease does not differ from the surgical treatment of primary pilonidal


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
a flap based procedure may be indicated following sinus excision with scarring, like the rhomboid flap (8, 9).


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
To the best of our knowledge, the existing literature is poor with studies comparing flap-based techniques with deep


[NarrativeText] (page 2)
suturing (or primary closure) techniques in the management of recurrent pilonidal disease.

The rhomboid flap Approach (32 patients)


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
The patient will be operated on under spinal anesthesia. The patients will be placed in prone jack-knife position on the


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
operating table with the legs slightly abducted and the buttocks strapped apart by adhesive tapes. The anus will be


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
excluded from the operative field by surgical drapes. The pathologic area to be excised will be marked. This area will be


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
enclosed by a rhombus shape with the long axis in the midline (ABCD). Lines AB = BC = CD = DA will be equal to


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
each other, with the axis AC being along the natal cleft. Line BD will be extended transversely and then the line EF will


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
be drawn equal in length. The rhombus containing the pilonidal sinus will be then excised down to the periosteum in the


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
midline and gluteal fascia laterally. Meticulous hemostasis will be done by means of electrocautery.


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
The flap will be dissected deep to the gluteal fascia (subfascial level) so as to raise thick a fasciocutaneous flap. This will


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
assure good vascularity of the flap without dead space. The rhomboid flap (CDEF) will be mobilized from the gluteal


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
fascia and sutured without tension in three layers (gluteal fascia with 2/0 Vicryl, subcutaneous fat with 3/0 Vicryl, and


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
the skin with 4/0 Prolene). As all sides will be equal in length, the flap fits in place without tension. A suction drain will


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
be left behind and the wound will be dressed as usual. Pressure wound dressing will be applied and removed on the third


[Title] (page 4)
postoperative day.


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
The deep suturing approach (32 patients)


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
A vertical elliptical incision encompassing all pilonidal pits will be made and excision of the sinus will be carried out


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
down to the level of the sacrococcygeal fascia. Tension will be released by a limited sharp dissection above the fascia.


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
After haemostasis is ensured using electrocautery, a suction drain will be inserted through a separate incision, then the


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
deep fascia will be approximated and the wound will be closed in layers using polyglactin 0 sutures. Finally, the skin will


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
be closed with 2/0 polypropylene interrupted mattress sutures.


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
Postoperative care and follow-up


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
Standard postoperative care, including mobilization and return to normal diet as quickly as possible will be


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
recommended. Early postoperative complications (bleeding and urine retention) will be noticed and recorded. Pain score


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
and early wound infection will be recorded. Post-operative pain will be assessed on the first, fourth postoperative day and


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
at stitch removal using a visual analogue scale (VAS) from zero (no pain) to 10 (worst pain imaginable) (10).


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
Patient will be discharged usually on the next day. Oral antibiotics and analgesics will be prescribed on patient’s


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
discharge. All the patients will be advised to shave the area around the operative site at least monthly.


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
All the patients will be recommended to visit the outpatient clinic twice weekly for two weeks then weekly for another


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
two weeks and then every 1 month during the follow-up period (6 months). During each visit, complete patient


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
assessment will be done. If any complications are detected (seroma, recurrence, wound dehiscence), it will be recorded as


[Title] (page 4)
well.


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
Infection will be considered as leakage of purulent secretion through the surgical wound and not only peri-incisional


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
hyperaemia. The suction drain will be removed when drainage became less than 20 mL per day (11). Seroma will be


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
defined as the formation of non-infected serous fluid collection beneath the flap and diagnosed by clinical examination


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
Duration of incapacity for work will be defined as the date on which patient returned to normal activities including


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
employment and leisure activities time from the date of surgery. In addition, patients will be asked to evaluate the


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
cosmetic appearance of the wound by looking at its picture using VAS ranging from 0-10 where 0 means the worst


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
cosmetic outcome and 10 indicates the best cosmetic outcome. A Turkish study used a 0 to 10 visual analogue scale


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
(VAS) scale to assess patient satisfaction with their cosmetic outcome (13).

[Title] (page 3)
Introduction


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
Rotator cuff tendinopathy represents the most common pathology of the shoulder, and


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
is the main reason for outpatient consultation for pathologies related to the shoulder


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
girdle.1,2 First-line treatment for rotator cuff tendinopathy usually consists of activity


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
stretching and strengthening exercises, oral


[Title] (page 3)
anti-inflammatory


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
medications.3-6 When these initial measures are not effective, a second line of


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
treatment consists of


[Title] (page 3)
local


[Title] (page 3)
infiltrations with corticosteroids.3-6 Corticosteroid


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
injections are often used as an inexpensive and effective treatment to reduce pain and


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
improve movement. However, most of the studies evaluating the effectiveness of


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
corticosteroids in patients with rotator cuff tendinopathy show that the clinical results


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
are usually short-lived.6-7 In addition, corticosteroids do not have any biological effect


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
that helps to regenerate or reverse the structural changes that occur in the rotator cuff


[Title] (page 3)
tendons as a consequence of chronic inflammation. 6-7


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
In this sense, platelet-rich plasma (PRP) emerges as a promising alternative due to its


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
ability to release pro-regenerative growth factors (FsC) and cytokines at the lesion site


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
(cite).8-9 Platelet Rich Plasma is considered a minimal handling product. It


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
commonly used in the United States, Europe and all of America. According to the


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
FDA it falls within the scope of section 361 (Public Health Service Law, Code 21 of


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
Federal Regulation 1271) of minimally manipulated therapies and according to


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
ANMAT under law 16463 and decree 9763/64. In addition, the centrifuge that will be


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
used in the following study at the Hospital Italiano de Buenos Aires is approved by


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
the ANMAT in (RegenLab disposition 13735). Specifically, at the Hospital Italiano de


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
Buenos Aires (HIBA), platelet-rich plasma has been used for more than 10 years for


[NarrativeText] (page 3)
is


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
different surgical procedures. Its use is framed within Rule 789; code 5851121. The


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
main indications for which PRP is used in the HIBA orthopedic service and for which


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
favorable results have been published are: muscle tears, diabetic foot ulcers, knee and


[Title] (page 4)
hip osteoarthritis, osteochondral knee lesions, partial lesions of the anterior cruciate


[Title] (page 4)
ligament, glenohumeral osteoarthritis and rotator cuff pathology.8-12


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
FsCs released by platelets have been shown to perform pro-regenerative functions in


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
vitro, including promoting stem and progenitor cell proliferation and recruitment, as


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
well as modulating inflammatory responses and stimulating angiogenesis.13-15


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
Furthermore, FsCs released by platelets have been shown not only to increase the


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
proliferation of rotator cuff-derived tenocytes but also to promote the production of


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
key extracellular matrix proteins, including collagen types I, II, and X; decorin;


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
aggrecan; and biglycan.14 In addition, PRP is also known to offer protection against


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
oxidative stress, which prevents cell apoptosis after injury, and also inhibits the


[Title] (page 4)
inflammatory effects of


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
interleukin-1b (IL1b), which can lead to cuff tendon


[Title] (page 4)
degeneration. rotator.15


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
Several small cohort studies and randomized controlled trials have examined the


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
clinical benefit of PRP for treating patients with rotator cuff tendinopathy, however


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
their results have been confounded by small patient samples, lack of a control group,


[Title] (page 4)
variable or insufficient data from the PRP preparations and short-term follow-up.16


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
clinical trials using


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
Different autologous PRP formulations to treat patients with rotator cuff tendinopathy


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
have yielded controversial results.16 Recently, Hurley et al.16 performed a systematic


[NarrativeText] (page 4)
review of randomized controlled trials on the use of PRP for the non-surgical


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
treatment of rotator cuff disease. Five randomized controlled trials met the inclusion


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
criteria, with 108 patients treated with PRP and 106 treated with a control. Two


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
studies found that PRP produced better outcomes, mainly pain scores, compared to a


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
control. One study showed no difference in clinical outcomes and two other studies


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
reported that PRP alone produced inferior results to control groups.16. Controlled trials


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
were needed to further investigate the benefits of PRP in early rotator cuff disease.16


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
Finally, regarding the safety of platelet-rich plasma, it has been shown to be a safe


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
product in multiple clinical trials. A recent Chocrane meta-analysis evaluated the


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
study included patients who had undergone surgery for multiple orthopedic conditions


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
including rotator cuff tears, anterior cruciate ligament tears, Achilles and patellar


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
tendinopathy, and epicondylitis. The percentage of complications associated with


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
treatment was 1.1% (12/1088 patients). In 15 of the 19 clinical trials there were no


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
adverse effects or complications related to PRP. Reported complications were


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
generally mild, including injection site hyperesthesia, local edema, and superficial


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
injection site infections. No severe complications associated with the procedure were


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
reported in any of the 1088 patients.17


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
The purpose of this study is to perform a double-blind randomized controlled trial,


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
comparing PRP with corticosteroids to provide pain relief and improve function in


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
patients with rotator cuff tendinopathy. The hypothesis of this study is that PRP would


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
provide improved pain relief and function in patients with rotator cuff tendinopathy


[NarrativeText] (page 5)
compared to standard treatment of CS injections.

To the patients of group 2: (Intervention) a subacromial infiltration with PRP will be

[NarrativeText] (page 9)
Masking of patients:


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
At the time of blood collection, patients will be placed in a hemotherapy extraction


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
chair with a screen that will prevent the patient from seeing their arm at the time of


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
blood collection. All patients will have a local anesthetic (cutaneous lidocaine) placed


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
in the crease of the elbow at the extraction site. The patients in the intervention group


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
will be injected with the extraction needle and 20 ml of blood will be obtained for the


[Title] (page 9)
preparation of the PRP.


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
On the other hand, the patients in the control group (corticosteroid) will be given


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
anesthetic and a dermal micro-puncture will be performed with the vacutainer


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
simulating blood extraction. After this, they will wait 1 minute, simulating the time it


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
takes for the extraction bag to fill with 20 ml.


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
In this way, we will ensure the masking of the treatment received, an essential


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
condition in this type of study since the scores used and validated for the clinical


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
evaluation of this pathology have a subjective component that could be altered or


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
biased if the patient knows which treatment received. On the other hand, the control


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
group, although it has active masking, only consists of a skin micropuncture with a


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
previous local anesthetic, which does not present complications or adverse effects and


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
may not be perceived by the patient due to the local anesthesia.


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
Both procedures will be carried out under antiseptic conditions by a Hemotherapy


[Title] (page 9)
technician in the procedure room of the Hemotherapy Service.


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
Subsequently, the patients will be accompanied to the procedure room where the


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
assigned treatment will be applied under ultrasound control by the principal


[NarrativeText] (page 9)
investigator. For patients in the CS group, 1 vial of 2 ml of Betamethasone will be


[NarrativeText] (page 10)
applied. The injection will be performed through a posterolateral approach. The CS


[NarrativeText] (page 10)
will infiltrate the subacromial bursa. For patients in the PRP group, a volume of 5 ml


[NarrativeText] (page 10)
will also be injected through a posterolateral approach. Patients will be informed


[NarrativeText] (page 10)
about possible adverse reactions to both injections. Patients will be allowed to


[NarrativeText] (page 10)
continue activity as tolerated after the injection and each patient will receive a specific


[NarrativeText] (page 10)
exercise program to do at home. Patients will not know what treatment they received,


[NarrativeText] (page 10)
meaning they will be blinded to the intervention. For this, at the time of injection, the


[NarrativeText] (page 10)
syringe will be covered with black insulating tape so that the patient cannot see the


[Title] (page 10)
contents of the syringe.


[NarrativeText] (page 10)
stage 3: Once the intervention is finished, all the patients will be rehabilitated by the

[NarrativeText] (page 10)
Elbow Society (ASES).21which is commonly used in the hospital.


[NarrativeText] (page 10)
patient will be objectively evaluated with the analogous visual scale, the ASES score,
